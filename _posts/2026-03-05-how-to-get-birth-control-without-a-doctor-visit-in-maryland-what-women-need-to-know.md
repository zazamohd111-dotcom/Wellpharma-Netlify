## Can a Pharmacist Really Prescribe Birth Control in Maryland?

Yes — with an important clarification. In Maryland, pharmacists who hold an additional certification are authorized to **prescribe hormonal contraceptives** under specific protocols. This authority falls under collaborative pharmacy practice and pharmacist prescribing legislation that has expanded significantly over recent years.

As of 2026, the Maryland Board of Pharmacy has recognized continuing education requirements specifically for pharmacists who prescribe contraception — which signals the formal recognition of this practice in our state. The details of what pharmacists can prescribe independently versus under collaborative agreements vary, and this is precisely why working with a certified, trained pharmacist matters.

## What Birth Control Options Are Available Through Pharmacy Prescribing?

Pharmacist-prescribed contraception typically includes hormonal methods. Here's what we can discuss and prescribe within our scope of practice:

### Combined Oral Contraceptives (The Pill)

The most commonly requested option. Combined pills contain both estrogen and progestin and are used for preventing pregnancy, regulating cycles, managing PMS, reducing acne, and managing endometriosis-related symptoms. There are dozens of formulations — and finding the right one for your body matters. We help match you to a formulation based on your history and any side effects you've experienced with previous pills.

### Progestin-Only Pills (The Mini Pill)

For women who can't take estrogen — including those who smoke and are over 35, have a history of blood clots, migraines with aura, or certain cardiovascular conditions — progestin-only pills offer effective contraception without estrogen exposure. They require stricter daily timing than combined pills but are an excellent option for the right patient.

### The Patch (Xulane / Twirla)

A weekly transdermal patch that delivers estrogen and progestin through the skin. A good option for women who prefer not to take a daily pill. Applied once weekly for three weeks, then a patch-free week. Similar efficacy and considerations to combined oral contraceptives.

### The Ring (NuvaRing / Annovera)

A flexible vaginal ring that releases hormones locally. NuvaRing is inserted for three weeks, removed for one. Annovera is a one-year ring that's inserted and removed on a monthly cycle. Both provide consistent hormone levels with lower peak estrogen than most pills.

### Emergency Contraception Counseling

We provide counseling on emergency contraception options — including Plan B (available over the counter) and prescription emergency contraception options — and can discuss the best choice based on timing and individual factors.

**Note:** Long-acting reversible contraceptives (IUDs and implants) require insertion by a trained clinician and are beyond the scope of a pharmacy visit. However, we can absolutely counsel you on these options and help you understand which might be worth pursuing with a gynecologist.
