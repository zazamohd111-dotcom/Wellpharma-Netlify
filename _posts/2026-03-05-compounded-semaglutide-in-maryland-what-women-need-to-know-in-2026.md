## What Is Compounded Semaglutide?

Semaglutide is the active molecule in two FDA-approved medications: **Ozempic** (approved for Type 2 diabetes management) and **Wegovy** (approved for chronic weight management in adults with obesity or overweight with a weight-related condition). Both contain the same active ingredient — they differ in dose and labeling.

**Compounded semaglutide** is that same molecule, prepared by a licensed 503A compounding pharmacy using pharmaceutical-grade active pharmaceutical ingredients (APIs). Rather than being mass-manufactured, it's compounded to a specific patient's prescription — their dose, their titration schedule, their clinical needs.

Here's the regulatory context you need: During the FDA shortage periods of 2023 through mid-2025, compounding pharmacies were explicitly permitted to compound semaglutide under FDA shortage provisions. As the commercial supply has improved, the FDA has updated its guidance — and this is where it gets nuanced.

**503A compounding pharmacies** (which is what WellPharma is) are permitted to compound medications for individual patients when there is a valid patient-specific prescription and a legitimate clinical reason. That includes situations where the commercial product doesn't meet a patient's specific needs due to dosing requirements, allergies, intolerances, or cost-related access barriers. This is patient-centered medicine, and it is legal when done correctly.

What the FDA warns against — and rightfully so — is compounding from facilities that don't follow USP standards, source ingredients from unverified suppliers, or operate without proper pharmacist oversight. Those operations exist, and they're the ones you should avoid.

## How Semaglutide Actually Works for Weight Loss

Semaglutide is a **GLP-1 receptor agonist** — it mimics glucagon-like peptide-1, a hormone your gut naturally releases after eating. Here's what that does in your body:

**It slows gastric emptying.** Food moves more slowly from your stomach to your small intestine, so you feel full significantly longer after meals. This single mechanism alone reduces caloric intake for most people without requiring constant tracking or willpower.

**It reduces appetite signals in the brain.** GLP-1 receptors exist in the hypothalamus — the region of your brain that regulates hunger and satiety. Semaglutide acts on these receptors directly, reducing appetite signals and dulling cravings, particularly for high-fat and high-sugar foods.

**It improves insulin sensitivity.** By improving how your cells respond to insulin, semaglutide stabilizes blood sugar and reduces the insulin spikes that promote fat storage. This is especially significant for perimenopausal women, whose insulin resistance increases by 20–30% as estrogen declines.

**It reduces inflammation.** Emerging research shows GLP-1 receptor agonists reduce systemic inflammation — itself a driver of weight gain, fatigue, and metabolic dysfunction that's very common in women over 40.

The clinical results from the landmark STEP-1 trial showed an average weight loss of **15.3% of body weight over 68 weeks** — a level previously associated only with bariatric surgery.

## Is Compounded Semaglutide Safe?

This is the question I hear most often, and it deserves a thorough, honest answer.

The semaglutide molecule itself has an extensive safety profile documented through large-scale FDA trials and years of real-world use. The safety question around compounded versions is really a question about **the quality of the pharmacy doing the compounding** — not the molecule.

Quality depends on three things: the source of the API, the standards used in preparation, and pharmacist oversight. Here's what matters:

✅ **Pharmaceutical-grade API** — Semaglutide sourced from FDA-registered facilities, verified for identity and purity, not from unregulated overseas suppliers.

✅ **USP 795 compliance** — The gold standard for non-sterile compounding. This means preparation in a controlled environment following strict pharmaceutical protocols, proper beyond-use dating, and documented batch records.

✅ **Precise measurement** — Accurate dosing equipment ensuring you receive the dose on your prescription, not an approximation.

✅ **Licensed pharmacist oversight** — Every compound reviewed and signed off by a PharmD before dispensing. Not a tech. Not an algorithm. A pharmacist.

✅ **Proper prescriber-patient relationship** — A valid prescription from a licensed provider who has reviewed your health history, not an online quiz.

The FDA's legitimate concern — and it's valid — is that not every operation compounding semaglutide meets these standards. There are med spas, online platforms, and unlicensed facilities cutting corners on ingredient sourcing or operating without proper oversight. Those are the ones that have generated warning letters and justified scrutiny.
