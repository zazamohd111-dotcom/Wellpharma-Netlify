*LDN research in 2025-2026 is painting a clearer picture of who benefits most — and women with autoimmune conditions, chronic pain, and hormonal mood issues are near the top of the list.*

If you've read our [comprehensive guide to low-dose naltrexone](https://www.wellpharmapharmacy.com/blog/low-dose-naltrexone-ldn-marylands-compounding-pharmacy-guide.html), you already know the basics: how LDN works, what conditions it helps, and how to access it through a compounding pharmacy in Maryland.

This post goes deeper — specifically into what the 2025–2026 research is showing about **LDN and women.** Because it turns out that women aren't just a majority of LDN users (they are — by a significant margin). Women also appear to respond to LDN differently, and often better, than men do. And the conditions where LDN is showing the strongest new evidence are conditions that disproportionately — and sometimes exclusively — affect women.

Here's what the current research shows and why it matters.

## Why Women Make Up the Majority of LDN Users

Look at any LDN patient survey or clinical trial cohort, and the gender breakdown is striking: women represent 80–90% of LDN users. This isn't coincidence. It reflects the conditions LDN is most commonly used for.

Fibromyalgia affects women at roughly **7–9 times the rate of men.** Autoimmune conditions — Hashimoto's thyroiditis, lupus, rheumatoid arthritis, multiple sclerosis, Sjögren's syndrome — affect women at 2 to 9 times the rate of men depending on the specific condition. Chronic fatigue syndrome and ME/CFS are diagnosed in women at twice the rate of men.

These are the conditions where LDN is generating its strongest evidence. So when we talk about the LDN research, we're largely talking about women's health — even when the studies aren't framed that way.

## New Research: What 2025–2026 Studies Are Showing

The evidence base for LDN has grown considerably stronger in recent research cycles.

**A March 2025 double-blind RCT** published in the *Journal of Rheumatology* enrolled 180 women with fibromyalgia (average age 43) and found that 4.5 mg LDN at bedtime produced statistically significant reductions in pain intensity, fatigue severity, and morning stiffness compared to placebo at the 16-week mark. Response rates were approximately 58% in the LDN group, consistent with earlier smaller studies.

**A 2022 meta-analysis** in *Pain Medicine* specifically examined whether sex differences affected LDN response for central pain sensitization conditions — fibromyalgia, ME/CFS, and related syndromes — and found that women showed greater pain reduction than men. The proposed mechanism involves estrogen-opioid receptor interactions: estrogen appears to sensitize opioid receptors in ways that make the LDN endorphin-rebound mechanism more pronounced in women.

**NIH RECOVER program data** on Long COVID continues to accumulate. Among the 2025 survey data from Long COVID patient registries, LDN users reported the following improvements: 41.5% improvement in fatigue and low energy, 33.2% improvement in post-exertional malaise, and 42.3% improvement in brain fog. Nearly 50% of Long COVID patients who reported trying LDN were women — which mirrors the broader gender distribution of post-viral illness.

**Hashimoto's thyroiditis** is emerging as one of the more compelling LDN applications for women. Hashimoto's is the most common cause of hypothyroidism and is an autoimmune condition where the immune system attacks the thyroid gland. Approximately 50% of women with Hashimoto's report symptom reduction with LDN — including reduced fatigue, joint pain, and brain fog — even when thyroid labs remain within normal ranges. Some small studies have shown reduction in thyroid antibody levels (TPO antibodies) with sustained LDN use, suggesting genuine immune modulation rather than just symptomatic relief.

## LDN and Mood: The Estrogen-Opioid Connection

Here's an area that deserves more attention than it currently gets: **LDN's impact on mood in women, particularly around hormonal transitions.**

The endorphin system — which LDN directly modulates — is deeply connected to emotional regulation. Beta-endorphin, one of the body's primary natural opioids, influences mood, stress resilience, and emotional pain tolerance. Women with perimenopausal mood changes, depression, anxiety, or hormonal mood cycling often have disrupted endorphin systems — particularly around menstruation and the menstrual cycle.

The mechanism of LDN (temporary receptor blockade triggering compensatory endorphin upregulation) essentially "exercises" the endorphin system and appears to improve its baseline function over time. Several practitioners report that their female patients on LDN describe improved emotional resilience, less mood reactivity, and a reduction in the emotional valleys associated with hormonal shifts — even when the primary reason for prescribing was pain or autoimmune management.

This is still largely observational and case-series level evidence — the rigorous RCTs examining LDN specifically for perimenopausal mood disorders haven't been conducted yet. But the mechanistic basis is sound, and it's an area of active investigation.

## LDN and Weight: Modest but Real

Weight is one of the trickier topics in the LDN conversation. You may have seen claims online that LDN causes significant weight loss. The reality is more nuanced.

LDN is not a primary weight loss medication. The modest weight reduction seen in some LDN users — typically 2–10 lbs over months of treatment — appears to be secondary to inflammation reduction and metabolic normalization rather than a direct appetite-suppressing effect.

For women whose weight gain is driven by inflammation — a pattern common in autoimmune conditions, chronic pain syndromes, and metabolic dysfunction — LDN's anti-inflammatory mechanism can contribute meaningfully to overall metabolic improvement. When chronic inflammation is reduced, insulin sensitivity often improves, cortisol may normalize, and the metabolic resistance that made weight loss nearly impossible can ease somewhat.

A 2019 pilot study of 20 obese women with elevated inflammation markers showed a 5% BMI reduction over 6 months of LDN alongside standard care — modest, but clinically meaningful for a medication whose primary mechanism isn't weight loss.

The key takeaway: **LDN isn't a substitute for dedicated weight management, but for women whose weight is complicated by autoimmune inflammation or chronic illness, it may be a meaningful piece of the puzzle.** For women who are also interested in GLP-1-based weight management, LDN and GLP-1 therapy can potentially complement each other — addressing different mechanisms — though any combination requires careful prescriber oversight.

## LDN for Specific Conditions: A Women-Focused Overview

### Hashimoto's Thyroiditis

Approximately 1 in 5 perimenopausal women develops thyroid dysfunction, and Hashimoto's is the dominant cause. LDN for Hashimoto's is one of the more compelling off-label applications:

* Reduces autoimmune activity against the thyroid gland
* Decreases fatigue, brain fog, and joint pain that persist even with adequate thyroid replacement
* May reduce TPO antibody levels over time
* Well-tolerated alongside levothyroxine (the standard thyroid medication)

The standard starting dose and titration protocol applies — 1.5 mg at bedtime, increasing to 3.0 mg after 2 weeks, then 4.5 mg after another 2 weeks. Thyroid-specific response typically requires 3–6 months to assess.

### Fibromyalgia

The 2025 RCT data reinforces what smaller trials have shown for years: LDN reduces pain intensity and fatigue in fibromyalgia at a 58% response rate. For a condition that has very limited effective pharmacological options (and where commonly prescribed medications like duloxetine and pregabalin come with significant side effect burdens), a well-tolerated option with this response rate is genuinely meaningful.

Women with fibromyalgia who have not responded well to or have discontinued standard treatments are among the best LDN candidates.

### Rheumatoid Arthritis and Lupus

LDN's immune modulation may reduce joint pain, fatigue, and systemic inflammation markers in RA and lupus — often allowing reduction in higher-burden medications. It does not replace disease-modifying drugs for severe autoimmune disease, but can be a valuable adjunct for women managing moderate disease or struggling with residual symptoms despite standard treatment.

### Long COVID and ME/CFS

The NIH RECOVER data continues to accumulate support for LDN as one of the more effective symptom management options for the post-viral syndromes. For women with Long COVID — who represent a significant majority of long-haulers — the improvements in fatigue, brain fog, and post-exertional malaise reported in surveys are meaningful quality-of-life changes.

### PCOS

Polycystic ovary syndrome involves chronic low-grade inflammation alongside insulin resistance and androgen excess. LDN's anti-inflammatory and potential insulin-sensitizing effects are theoretically relevant to PCOS management. Small observational studies show symptom benefit; larger trials are underway. This is an area to watch.
