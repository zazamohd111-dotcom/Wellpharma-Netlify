*You're not 22 anymore — and your birth control options have evolved. A pharmacist explains what works, what's safer, and what to reconsider after 35.*

There's a common misconception that once women reach their late 30s or early 40s, birth control becomes less important. The thinking goes: fertility declines, so the risk of pregnancy must be too low to worry about.

Here's the reality: **women over 35 still have a meaningful pregnancy risk until one full year after their last menstrual period.** And in perimenopause — that hormonal transition that can last 8–10 years — cycles become irregular and unpredictable, making it genuinely hard to know when ovulation is still happening.

At the same time, the safety profile of certain contraceptive options does change after 35, particularly for women who smoke, have high blood pressure, or carry other cardiovascular risk factors that become more prevalent with age.

So what's the right birth control for women over 35? The answer isn't the same for everyone — but here's a clear framework to work from.

## Why Contraceptive Choice Changes After 35

Two things happen simultaneously for most women in their mid-to-late 30s and beyond:

**1. Fertility declines, but doesn't disappear.** Natural fertility decreases significantly after 35 — the probability of conception per cycle is lower than it was at 25. But "lower" is not the same as "zero," and irregular perimenopausal cycles can make it impossible to predict ovulation. Unintended pregnancy rates in women over 40 remain significant, and pregnancies at this stage carry higher risks (including chromosomal issues, gestational complications, and maternal health considerations) that make reliable contraception genuinely important.

**2. Health risk factors accumulate.** Blood pressure tends to rise with age. Cardiovascular risk increases. Smoking history becomes more relevant. Migraine patterns change. These factors directly affect which contraceptive methods are appropriate — some forms of hormonal birth control that were perfectly fine at 22 carry a meaningfully different risk profile at 42.

This is exactly why birth control choices for women over 35 deserve a real clinical conversation — not just a refill of whatever you've been taking for years.

## The Safety Question Around Estrogen After 35

The key safety consideration for women over 35 is the use of **estrogen-containing contraceptives** (combined oral contraceptives, the patch, and the combined vaginal ring).

Estrogen raises the risk of venous thromboembolism (blood clots), stroke, and heart attack. In young, healthy, non-smoking women with normal blood pressure, that absolute risk increase is small and generally accepted. But as women age and accumulate cardiovascular risk factors, that calculus changes.

The World Health Organization's and CDC's Medical Eligibility Criteria for Contraceptive Use — the clinical guidelines used by healthcare providers worldwide — flag the following as situations where estrogen-containing contraceptives require careful evaluation or avoidance for women over 35:

* **Smoking (≥15 cigarettes/day) in women over 35** — This combination significantly elevates stroke and heart attack risk. Combined hormonal contraceptives are generally contraindicated.
* **Hypertension (blood pressure ≥160/100)** — Estrogen can raise blood pressure further and increase cardiovascular risk.
* **Migraines with aura** — Associated with increased stroke risk; estrogen-containing contraceptives are generally contraindicated.
* **History of blood clots, stroke, or heart attack** — Strong contraindication.
* **Diabetes with vascular complications** — Increased cardiovascular risk.
* **Obesity (BMI ≥35) combined with other risk factors** — Warrants careful evaluation.

None of this means women over 35 can't use hormonal birth control. It means the choice of which hormonal method requires more individualized screening — which is exactly what a proper consultation provides.

## The Best Options for Women Over 35 (Ranked by Evidence)

Here's how current clinical guidelines from ACOG (2025) and the CDC rank contraceptive options for women in this age group:

### 1. Hormonal IUDs (Mirena, Kyleena, Liletta, Skyla) — Highly Recommended

Hormonal IUDs are the gold standard for women over 35 for several reasons:

* **No systemic estrogen.** Levonorgestrel (the progestin in these IUDs) works locally in the uterus with minimal systemic absorption. This eliminates the cardiovascular risks associated with estrogen-containing methods.
* **Exceptional efficacy.** >99% effective — comparable to sterilization, without being permanent.
* **Manages perimenopausal symptoms.** Hormonal IUDs dramatically reduce heavy and irregular bleeding that's common in perimenopause — up to 90% reduction in menstrual blood loss. For women whose cycles become chaotic in their 40s, this is often a significant quality-of-life benefit.
* **Long duration.** Mirena lasts up to 8 years; Kyleena and Liletta up to 5–6 years. One insertion covers the remainder of your reproductive years.
* **Reversible.** Remove it, and fertility returns.

### 2. Progestin-Only Pills (Mini Pill — Norethindrone, Slynd) — Strong Option

Progestin-only pills are an excellent choice for women who:

* Smoke over 35
* Have a history of blood clots or cardiovascular risk factors
* Have migraines with aura
* Are breastfeeding
* Simply prefer to avoid estrogen

The traditional mini pill (norethindrone) requires taking it at the same time every day (within a 3-hour window) to maintain efficacy. A newer formulation, **Slynd** (drospirenone progestin-only pill), has a 24-hour missed dose window — making it much more forgiving for busy women and often preferred for its more flexibility.

Progestin-only pills may cause irregular spotting, particularly in the first few months. For most women, this settles down over time.

### 3. The Implant (Nexplanon) — Excellent Long-Term Option

A small, flexible rod inserted under the skin of the upper arm. Nexplanon releases etonogestrel (a progestin) steadily for up to 3–5 years. Like hormonal IUDs:

* Contains no estrogen
* > 99% effective
* Suitable for women with cardiovascular risk factors or estrogen contraindications
* Insertion and removal are quick in-office procedures

The main caveat: irregular bleeding patterns, particularly in the first year. Some women have no bleeding at all (welcomed by many). Others have unpredictable spotting. If menstrual predictability matters to you, a hormonal IUD may be preferable.

### 4. Combined Oral Contraceptives (Low-Dose) — Still an Option for Many Women

For healthy non-smoking women over 35 with normal blood pressure and no cardiovascular risk factors, low-dose combined oral contraceptives remain a reasonable choice. In fact, they offer specific benefits in perimenopause:

* Regulate the irregular cycles that characterize perimenopause
* Reduce hot flashes and vasomotor symptoms
* Protect bone density (estrogen maintains bone mass)
* Reduce ovarian cyst formation
* Manage premenstrual symptoms

The key phrase is **low-dose** — modern formulations contain much lower estrogen than older pills, significantly reducing cardiovascular risk compared to what your mother's generation was taking.

### 5. Copper IUD (Paragard) — Hormone-Free Option

For women who want to avoid all hormones entirely, the copper IUD (Paragard) is the most effective non-hormonal long-term option available. It works by creating a toxic environment for sperm via copper ions, with no hormonal activity.

Paragard lasts up to 10–12 years and can also be used as emergency contraception if inserted within 5 days of unprotected sex.

The main downside for perimenopausal women: copper IUDs often increase menstrual bleeding and cramping — which is the opposite of what most women in this stage want. For women who already have heavy or irregular cycles, this is usually not the right choice.

## A Note on When to Stop Birth Control

This is one of the most common questions we get from women in their late 40s: **"When can I stop using birth control?"**

The standard clinical guidance is:

* Women under 50: Continue contraception for **2 full years** after the last menstrual period
* Women 50 and over: Continue for **1 full year** after the last menstrual period

The challenge is that in perimenopause, periods become irregular and you won't always know if a "missed period" is the start of menopause or just a long cycle. Hormone testing can help — measuring FSH (follicle-stimulating hormone) and estradiol levels can give you a clearer picture of where you are in the transition.
